Quantitative Risk Management App

Role: Quantitative Analyst @ Dalhousie Investment Society (DALIS)

Challenge: The Dalhousie Investment Society, managing a multi-asset portfolio, had a significant operational blind spot: they had no viable or easily accessible way to track their Profit and Loss (PNL). This prevented the teams from accurately measuring performance, managing risk, and making data-driven strategic decisions.

My Solution: I built a quantitative analysis application from the ground up in Python. The app features a Tkinter GUI for user interaction and leverages the Bloomberg Python API to pull raw trade ticket data. Using Pandas and NumPy, it processes this data to calculate and track daily PNL for each of the four investment teams and the society's total portfolio. I also integrated advanced risk management tools, including Monte Carlo simulations and stock correlation matrices, which are visualized with Matplotlib.

Results: The application I developed became the central tool for performance tracking within the society, providing critical, on-demand PNL data for the first time. By introducing quantitative tools like hedging analysis and Monte Carlo simulations, I elevated the society's analytical capabilities, enabling more sophisticated risk management and data-informed investment strategies.

High-Efficiency Liquid Chip Cooling Design

Course: Advanced Heat Transfer @ Dalhousie University

Challenge: A multi-chip module generating over 200W of heat required a high-performance liquid cooling solution to prevent thermal throttling. The goal was to design a compact and efficient cold plate that could significantly outperform baseline cooling methods and maintain a stable operating temperature under a heavy load.

My Solution: I designed and simulated a custom liquid cold plate using CFD/thermal analysis software. The design features a copper manifold to maximize thermal conductivity from the chips to a microchannel heat sink containing 26 optimized aluminum fins. I engineered a streamlined internal fluid domain to ensure even flow distribution and maximize the convective heat transfer coefficient across the entire surface area.

Results: The final simulated design successfully dissipated over 707W of heat, a 353.9% improvement in thermal efficiency compared to the original 200W design goal. This was achieved while keeping the maximum chip temperature stable below 85°C, allowing for significantly higher and more sustained performance from the electronics.
